\section*{Introduction}

The goal of these notes is to show non-relativistic quantum mechanics can be closely
approximated by classical dynamics with probabilistic initial data, along with an interesting
transformation of coordinates. The approximation may be so close as to be indistinguishable.
We give the notes in terms of one spatial dimension, but it is trivial to extend to multiple
dimensions.

\section*{Quantum Perspective}

A tiny one-dimensional non-relativistic particle of mass $M$ in a tiny potential $V(x)$.
Here the accepted model is the Schr\"odinger equation, where $\Psi(x,t)$ gives the probability
of observing the particle at location $x$ at time $t$. The wave function $\Psi(x,t)$
evolves according to the partial differential equation
\[
i\hbar \Psi_t = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2M}\Psi_{xx} + V(x)\Psi
\]
joule-seconds. $\hbar \approx 1.05 \times 10^{-34}$.

\section*{Classical Perspective}

A regular one-dimensional non-relativistic particle of mass $M$ in a regular potential $V(x)$.
The position of the particle $x(t)$ and momentum of the particle $p(t)$ evolves according to
the ordinary differential equation
\[
M \frac{dx}{dt} = p \qquad \frac{dp}{dt} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}.
\]

\section*{Classical From Quantum Perspective}

There should be a way to see classical mechanics arrive as an approximation of quantum
mechanics. This should not be surprising, and is included here for completeness.

\medskip
\noindent
Approximate:
\[
\Psi \approx A \exp\left(\frac{iS}{\hbar}\right)
\]
Keeping the highest order terms
\[
-S_t = \frac{1}{2M}\left(S_x\right)^2 + V
\]
Differentiate with respect to $x$:
\[
-S_{xt} = \frac{1}{M}S_x S_{xx} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\]
Now define $P = S_x$
\[
-P_t = \frac{1}{M}P P_x + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\]
This is a first order hyperbolic differential equation and is solvable by the method of
characteristics. $p(t) = P(x(t),t)$
\[
\frac{dp}{dt} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial P}{\partial t}
= \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} - \frac{1}{M}P P_x - \frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\]
Choosing
\[
\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{1}{M}p \qquad \frac{dp}{dt} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\]
reduces the equation. So, to leading order, the Schr\"odinger equation carries phase-gradient
information along characteristics defined by the equations of motion for classical mechanics.

\medskip
\noindent
$M \frac{dx}{dt} = p$, $\frac{dp}{dt} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}$,
$x(0) = x_0$, $p(0) = p_0$.

\medskip
\noindent
Along the characteristics the phase gradient evolves as the classical hamiltonian system.
Note that characteristics will inevitably be singular.

\section*{Quantum From Probabilistic Classical Perspective}

So what has to be added to classical mechanics to get quantum mechanics? This seems more
interesting. You could reverse the WKB approximation above, but that is not particularly
enlightening. We instead combine the following concepts.

\subsection*{1. Any finite dimensional ordinary differential equation in $\mathbb{R}^n$ can be embedded as partial differential equations}

These partial differential equations describe the probability of a current value given an initial
probability distribution.

\subsection*{2. The Fourier transform}

\[
\text{a. }\Psi(k,t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-ikx}\Psi(x,t)\,dx
\]
\[
\text{b. }\partial_x \Psi \rightarrow i k \Psi
\qquad
\text{c. }x\Psi \rightarrow i\partial_k \Psi
\]
We use $k = p/\hbar$ which compresses the spectrum greatly.

\subsection*{3. The numerical approximation}

\[
f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+\varepsilon/2) - f(x-\varepsilon/2)}{\varepsilon}
+ O(\varepsilon^2)
\]

\subsection*{Using Concept 1: ODE $\Rightarrow$ PDE}

Big Idea: Think of an ordinary differential equation initial value problem as evolving a
partial differential equation which describes the probability distribution of the initial values.

\medskip
\noindent
ODE
\[
\frac{dx_1}{dt} = v_1 \qquad \frac{dx_2}{dt} = v_2 \qquad \cdots \qquad \frac{dx_n}{dt} = v_n
\]
PDE
\[
\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}
+ \frac{\partial (v_1 f)}{\partial x_1}
+ \frac{\partial (v_2 f)}{\partial x_2}
+ \cdots
+ \frac{\partial (v_n f)}{\partial x_n}
= 0
\]
Using the dirac delta function for the initial value, the PDE recovers the ODE initial value
problem: $f(x,0) = f_0(x) = \delta(x-x_0)$.

For the hamiltonian system
\[
\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{1}{M}p \qquad \frac{dp}{dt} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\]
\[
\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}
+ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{1}{M}pf\right)
+ \frac{\partial}{\partial p}\left(-\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}f\right)
= 0
\]

\subsection*{Using concept 2: Momentum Transform}

Now Fourier transform only with respect to the variable $p$ as follows
\[
f(x,p,t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}
e^{-i p \tilde{p}/\hbar} f(x,\tilde{p},t)\,d\tilde{p}
\]
Now the PDE becomes
\[
i\hbar f_t - \frac{\hbar^2}{M} f_{xp} + p \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} f = 0
\]

\subsection*{Using Concept 3: Variable Change and Only Approximation}

Now change variables:
\[
z^{+} = x + \frac{p}{2}, \qquad z^{-} = x - \frac{p}{2}
\]
So
\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z^+} + \frac{\partial}{\partial z^-},
\qquad
\frac{\partial}{\partial p} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial}{\partial z^+}
- \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial}{\partial z^-}
\]
And use the approximation
\[
\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\approx \frac{V(x+p/2) - V(x-p/2)}{p}
\]
\[
i\hbar f_t - \frac{\hbar^2}{2M}
\left(\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^{+2}} - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^{-2}}\right)f
+ [V(z^+) - V(z^-)]f = 0
\]
While this does not yet look like Schr\"odinger\textquotesingle s equation, this is a linear differential
equation, and we can look at separation of variables to construct a general solution to this.
Suppose $f = \sum \Psi^+(z^+)\Psi^-(z^-)$ where each of these terms satisfy the above, we have
\[
\pm i\hbar \Psi_t^{\pm} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2M}\Psi_{zz}^{\pm} + V\Psi^{\pm} = 0
\]
Where $z$ takes the respective roles of $z^+$ and $z^-$. For real potential $V$, we can have
$\Psi^+ = \Psi_\lambda$ and $\Psi^- = \Psi_\lambda^*$ where the $\Psi_\lambda$ are eigenvectors
of the original Schr\"odinger\textquotesingle s equation. These form a complete basis for for the
solution of PDE for $f$.

\medskip
\noindent
$f(t,z^+,z^-) = \sum_{\lambda,\lambda'} C_{\lambda,\lambda'}
e^{i(\lambda-\lambda')t}\Psi_\lambda(z^+)\Psi_{\lambda'}^*(z^-)$.

This has a most compelling DC component, which seems too surprising to just be lucky. In
particular along $p=0$
\[
\text{DC}[f](x) = \sum_\lambda C_\lambda |\Psi_\lambda(x)|^2
\]
The only approximation here is the centered difference estimate:
\[
p \frac{\partial V}{\partial x}
\approx V\left(x+\frac{p}{2}\right) - V\left(x-\frac{p}{2}\right)
= p \frac{\partial V}{\partial x} + \frac{p^3}{24}\frac{\partial^3 V}{\partial x^3} + \cdots
\]
Looking at the definition of the transform, this is an excellent approximation since it is
compressed by a factor of $\hbar$. Note this is exact for the harmonic oscillator and very good
for all small momentum spectra ($p$ is the transform of the momentum). I\textquotesingle m not
certain there is an experiment that can determine the difference between this and the
Schr\"odinger equation. The plank constant makes an extreme compression of the spectrum.

Starting from a dirac- initial condition for $f$ at a specific position and momentum, we have
$f = \delta(x-x_0)e^{i(p-p_0)/\hbar}$ and so $C_\lambda = \Psi_\lambda(x_0)e^{i(p-p_0)/\hbar}$.
